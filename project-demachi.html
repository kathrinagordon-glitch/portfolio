Demachi Yanagi Station

A rooftop intervention at the edge of transit and place

"A station is not just a transport node — it is one of the most human spaces in a city. People arrive, wait, meet, get lost, and find their way. The design should respond to all of that."

— Katharina Gordon, project concept statement

Hero image — night render

Demachi Yanagi is the terminal station of both the Eizan Electric Railway and the Keihan Line — one above ground, one below. Every day, thousands of commuters, students, tourists, and locals pass through it. Many never stop.

This project asks: what would it mean to design a station that people actually want to be in? Not just pass through — but arrive at. The rooftop intervention creates a new layer above the transit infrastructure — a public space that belongs to the neighbourhood as much as to the railway.

I know this station from the inside.

I arrived at Demachi Yanagi as a tourist with a paper map. I came back as a student locking my bicycle in the rain. I passed through it every morning as a commuter with eight minutes to spare. And eventually I stopped there as a local — for no reason at all. Four different relationships with the same space. Each time, the station offered nothing back. It moved you through. That was all. This project is an attempt to design the station I kept wishing existed.

The Problem

"The space functions as a corridor, not a place."

Site map — people flow diagram (image e)

Transit spaces are designed for efficiency. They prioritize movement over experience — and at Demachi Yanagi, this creates compounding problems. The existing station treats its users as vehicles to be processed rather than people with time, attention, and a desire to be somewhere.

Three specific failures define the space: the station offers almost no reason to slow down or pause; the split between underground and above-ground levels creates disorientation without clear spatial logic; and the four user groups who share the space — commuters, students, locals, and tourists — move in parallel without any designed opportunity to meet.

Who Uses This Space

The Commuter

Knows the station well. Moves through it every day on autopilot. Values efficiency above everything. Has no reason to stop — unless something in the space gives them one.

The Student

Uses the station as a waypoint between campus, the river path, and home. Often has a few minutes to spare. Open to a seat, a view, or an unexpected pause. Sensitive to atmosphere over function.

The Local

Uses the station daily without always taking a train. Locks their bicycle here, takes a shortcut through, buys coffee on the way home. Their relationship to the space is habitual, territorial, and deeply personal.

The Tourist

Arrives by Keihan from Gion or Fushimi, transfers to Eizan to reach Kurama or Kibune. Carries a map, moves slowly, looks up. Needs clear wayfinding, a moment to orient, and a spatial experience that already feels like Kyoto before the train even departs.

Design Response

The proposal introduces a rooftop layer — a publicly accessible platform above the station concourse, reached by a wide staircase that announces itself from street level. This is not a utilitarian roof: it is a designed destination.

The spatial logic follows three intentions. First, pause over passage: the rooftop creates a reason to stop, with framed views to the Kamo and Takano rivers, sheltered seating, and open areas for gathering. Second, legibility: the roof structure — visible from both above and below — acts as an orientation marker across the station's split levels. Third, threshold: the staircase from street to roof creates a gradual transition from movement to stillness, from transit to place.

The Roof System

The roof is the project's central structural and conceptual move. A W-truss system supports a canopy of fractal shade panels — triangular modules derived from the geometry of the willow leaf that names the area. The panels respond to sun angle and temperature, opening and closing automatically throughout the day.

The form is drawn from Mandelbrot's definition of fractal geometry: a structure that appears the same at every scale. Like a willow leaf, the panel subdivides into smaller versions of itself — creating a surface that is structurally rigid but visually weightless. Under the canopy, the light shifts constantly. The station looks different at eight in the morning than it does at four in the afternoon.

From Willow to Structure

The willow leaf as structural and formal origin

The name Demachi Yanagi means standing willows. The fractal geometry of the roof is a direct translation of the willow leaf: a self-similar form that repeats at every scale, appearing light and organic while providing structural rigidity.

Spatial Layers

Station programme — three vertical layers

The project organises space across three vertical layers. At ground level, the existing station flow continues — efficient, legible, and largely unchanged. An intermediate landing, set half a level above street, creates the first pause point: wide enough to linger, open enough to see across the station, shaded enough to feel sheltered.

The rooftop is the third layer and the main destination. From here, the rivers are visible on both sides. The city spreads north toward the mountains. The fractal canopy filters the light above. Below, the trains arrive and depart. The roof belongs to no particular user type — it is public space in the strictest sense: available to anyone who climbs the stair.

Process

The design developed through site observation, people flow mapping, and iterative physical modelling. The first site visits tracked how users actually moved through the station — where they paused, where they accelerated, where they looked confused. Circulation maps showed the gaps: underused corners, missed views, disconnected levels.

Physical models tested the structural logic of the W-truss and the fractal panel system at 1:50. Each iteration adjusted the canopy density, the stair geometry, and the relationship between the rooftop edge and the surrounding streets. The process moved between sketch, model, and section drawing in fast cycles, each one tightening the connection between structural ambition and spatial experience.

Reflection

This project taught me that transit spaces are already UX problems — they just aren't always framed that way. People feel lost, rushed, or invisible not because of their own failings but because the space gives them no other option.

Designing the roof was the moment I understood that architecture and interaction design are asking the same question: how does this person feel right now, and what does the environment do to help them? A commuter who can see the river from a rooftop, even for thirty seconds, has had a different day. That is what design is for.

That question — how do we build environments that serve the full range of a person, not just their most efficient self — is what led me from architecture toward interaction design. The tools change. The question stays the same.
